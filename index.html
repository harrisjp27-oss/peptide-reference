The Peptide Reference

A working map of the peptides (and a few non-peptides that travel with them) people actually use or debate — graded honestly by how much real human testing exists, not by how loudly they're marketed. Most of this list has never been through a controlled human trial. The color of each compound tells you where it really stands.

Read this first

This is an information reference, not medical advice and not an endorsement. The dosing and frequency figures below are the conventions reported in the literature or clinic/vendor sources — for the unapproved compounds they are not validated, not standardized, and not safe to assume. Realities that cut across almost the entire list: (1) Most of these are sold as "research chemicals" with no purity, sterility, or dose guarantee — contamination with endotoxin or mis-sequenced peptide is a documented problem. (2) In late 2023 the FDA placed ~17–19 of these in Category 2 (barred from compounding); a Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee review of 7 of them is scheduled for July 23–24, 2026, and an HHS statement in Feb 2026 floated moving ~14 back to Category 1 — announced, not finalized. (3) Almost everything that promotes tissue growth or new blood-vessel formation carries a theoretical cancer-acceleration concern that has not been ruled out in humans. (4) Nearly all are banned by WADA, and the U.S. DoD adopts the WADA list — relevant if you're ever drug-tested for sport, employment, or clearance.
How the evidence tiers work, and how to read the conflicts

Each compound gets a single grade for evidence maturity — how far it has actually been tested in humans — plus a ▲ flag when there's a specific, non-trivial safety signal beyond the general cautions above.

AApproved & robust. FDA or another major regulator approved it, backed by large randomized trials. The known quantity. BStrong human data. Substantial human trials (Phase 2/3) but not approved for this use, or approved only outside the US with good data. CLimited human data. Small or dated human studies, foreign approval with little Western replication, or an approved drug used far off-label. DPreclinical only. Animal and test-tube data; little or no human evidence. This is where most "research peptides" actually sit. EMinimal / cautionary. Essentially anecdotal, or development was halted, or the headline evidence was retracted.

On disagreements: sources conflict most on (a) whether a compound causes cancer or just theoretically could, (b) "standard" doses (vendor/clinic numbers vary widely and aren't trial-derived), and (c) regulatory status, which is actively shifting in 2026. Where a specific claim is contested, it's called out in the compound's Evidence & disputes field. Vendor and telehealth pages — which make up a large share of what's searchable — consistently overstate benefit and understate uncertainty; they were used for dosing conventions and naming, while clinical, regulatory, and PubMed sources were weighted for efficacy and safety claims.
